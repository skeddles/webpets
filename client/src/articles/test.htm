The Goal

There‚Äôs a lot of different things to keep in mind while creating art. You‚Äôll learn more things to consider the more classes you take, and the more you progress on your artistic journey. But there‚Äôs one fundamental goal in pixel art that should be considered before all others, and that‚Äôs what this lesson is about.

Your goal is to create an image that can be understood by the people who view it.

If you are creating a portrait of a dog, your #1 goal is to make sure that anyone who views it can easily identify the subject as a dog. Great texture or shading wont matter if your viewers are confused on what the subject is. It‚Äôs almost always a good idea to sacrifice detail and complexity for simplicity and clarity.

This goes for all parts of your image too, not just the main subject. Small details can add a lot of depth and flavor to your image, they can even aid in making more important subjects easier to understand. But if a smaller subject isn‚Äôt easily easily understood, it does the opposite: it confuses and distracts the viewer from the important parts.

So your goal is not to continuously add detail, but to continuously refine your image until it‚Äôs as easy to understand as possible. This frequently means simplifying or removing details, even if they‚Äôre good ideas or realistic.

This concept is especially important in pixel art, since the restrictive nature of drawing with squares can make it hard to draw small details. In general, you have to tune your amount of detail to the canvas size, and if the canvas is too small to properly depict something, then you gotta leave it out.

Pixel art is a simplification, similar to cartoons or graphic design, and it must be created with intentional purpose and clarity in mind.

It‚Äôs important that you understand that the process of pixel art is somewhat different than drawing - you aren‚Äôt trying to perform the perfect flick-of-the-wrist to get your lines perfect ‚Äî perfection in pixel art comes from refinement. Recognizability & Simplicity

So if clarity in our depictions is our biggest goal, how do we achieve that? You might think that in order to help the viewer recognize something, you should give them as many clues as possible, by adding more details. But that‚Äôs not really how our brains work. When we look at something, we can understand what it is nearly instantly, and completely unconsciously. There‚Äôs no time to process lots of little details, and most of our perception is based on a quick and vague snapshot. It will take a few seconds of looking around the piece and processing each detail separately, and by that time, you‚Äôll have already developed a vague idea, to which the details build upon.

The Tools

Now you understand the goal and the process, all that‚Äôs left is to pick up your tools, and start creating. Most pixel artists use software designed specifically for making pixel art, called pixel editors. It‚Äôs also possible to use general drawing/image editing programs such as photoshop (called raster graphics editors), though they will also contain many tools not suited for pixel art. We have a list of recommended software in our #resources channel, as well as on the Lospec website, but you can also ask here for guidance if you need help picking one.

Pixel art software can contains hundreds of tools, but there is one that stands far above the others, as the most important tool. Every single pixel art image could be created with this one tool, all other tools just make your process easier/faster, as long as you master this one first: the pencil tool.

As long as you can locate your pencil tool, you can create pixel art. The most simple pixel art workflow loop looks like this:

  1. select a color
  1. place pixels of that color

You can repeat these two step over and over until your piece is complete. As you learn more techniques and tools you‚Äôll add more steps to your process, but for now it‚Äôs best to master the basics.

What‚Äôs great about pixel art is that it‚Äôs so forgiving, and infinitely editable. You don‚Äôt have to get your lines right on your first try, you can just get the general shapes down, and then tweak and perfect it for as long as you need to until you‚Äôre satisfied.

Summary
